## Problem 4 — Scenario modeling (60 min)

The board wants to know what it would take to fix `paid_search`. In `scenarios.py`, model three independent scenarios (change one variable at a time, holding the others at their observed values) and report the resulting LTV:CAC ratio for each:

1. **Cut CAC.** What if `paid_search`'s fully-loaded CAC dropped 25% (better ad targeting, same team) — new CAC, new ratio?
2. **Cut churn.** What if `paid_search`'s average monthly churn dropped from ~8.9% to 5% (better onboarding) — new LTV, new ratio?
3. **Raise price.** What if `paid_search`'s MRR rose from $149 to $179/month (price increase, same churn, same CAC) — new margin, new LTV, new ratio?

**Deliver** `scenarios.py` printing all three new ratios, plus a two-sentence note on which single lever gets `paid_search` closest to the 3:1 healthy threshold on its own.

## Problem 5 — Add a third channel (60 min)

1. Design and insert 8 new `customers` rows on the `referral` channel: pick a `signup_month`, `mrr` (reuse $99 or $149, your call), and `churn_month`/`NULL` for each — make at least 6 of the 8 still active, reflecting referral's typically strong retention.

3. Compute `referral`'s fully-loaded CAC, margin-based LTV (either method), and LTV:CAC ratio using the same queries/functions you built this week.
4. Then **undo** it cleanly: `DELETE FROM customers WHERE channel = 'referral'; DELETE FROM channel_costs WHERE channel = 'referral';` and confirm the counts are back to 64 / 24.

**Deliver** `referral-channel.sql` (the inserts, the analysis queries and their output, and the cleanup) plus one sentence on whether your invented `referral` channel beat both `paid_search` and `organic_content` on LTV:CAC — and whether that surprised you.
