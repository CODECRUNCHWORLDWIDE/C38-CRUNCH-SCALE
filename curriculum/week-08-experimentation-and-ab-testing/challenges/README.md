# Week 8 — Challenges

Two open-ended problems. Unlike the exercises, these have **no single right answer** — the skill is judgment, defended with math and SQL, not recall. Do them after the three exercises.

1. **[Challenge 1 — Design a full experiment](challenge-01-design-a-full-experiment.md)** — take a vague growth idea from a one-sentence pitch to a launch-ready experiment spec, sized and all. *(~90 min.)*
2. **[Challenge 2 — Catch a broken test](challenge-02-catch-a-broken-test.md)** — a "clean win" write-up has four invalidating problems baked into it. Find all four, and know which ones are fatal versus fixable. *(~90 min.)*

## How these are judged

There's no answer key with a single correct number. Instead, each challenge tells you what a *strong* submission looks like. You're being graded on:

- **Correctness of the math** — every sample-size, z-test, or chi-square number you report is actually computed correctly from the numbers given.
- **Completeness of judgment** — for Challenge 1, does the spec cover every element from Lecture 1's brief structure, with real, defensible numbers, not placeholders? For Challenge 2, did you find every planted problem, not just the obvious one?
- **Honest severity ranking** — not every flaw in an experiment is fatal. Strong submissions distinguish "this invalidates the whole result, stop" from "this is a real concern, but here's how you'd account for it and still draw a conclusion."

Keep your work in `challenge-01.md` / `challenge-02.md` with your numbers **and** your written reasoning. The reasoning is the point — a number alone, without the argument for why it's the right call, is an incomplete answer.
